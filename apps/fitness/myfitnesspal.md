## 1. Overview & History

### Founding Story (2005)

MyFitnessPal was co-founded in 2005 by brothers **Mike Lee** and **Albert Lee**, Korean Americans who grew up in Schenectady, New York. Mike graduated from Princeton (economics) and was working toward medical school at UCLA when he needed to lose weight for his wedding. Told to count calories, he found every existing tool terrible -- so he taught himself to code and built MyFitnessPal himself. He then called his brother Albert (UC Berkeley economics grad, former Yahoo and GE Capital) and "pulled the big brother card," convincing Albert to quit his job and join.

The brothers **bootstrapped for 8 years** without raising any outside capital. At one point they had just 4 employees serving 30 million active website users. This extreme capital efficiency is one of the most remarkable bootstrapping stories in consumer tech.

### Under Armour Acquisition (2015) -- $475M

In February 2015, Under Armour acquired MyFitnessPal for **$475 million** as part of a broader "Connected Fitness" strategy that also included MapMyFitness and Endomondo. At the time of acquisition, MyFitnessPal had ~80 million registered users. Under Armour's vision was to create a digital health ecosystem, but the integration never fully materialized and the company struggled to monetize the platform effectively.

### Sale to Francisco Partners (2020) -- $345M

On October 30, 2020, Under Armour announced the sale of MyFitnessPal to private equity firm **Francisco Partners for $345 million** -- a **$130 million loss** from the original purchase price. The sale closed in December 2020. Under Armour simultaneously shut down Endomondo (by end of 2021) and refocused on its core apparel business.

Under Francisco Partners' ownership, MyFitnessPal has undergone a significant transformation:
- Aggressive monetization (moving features behind paywall)
- Introduction of tiered Premium/Premium+ pricing
- AI-powered features (Meal Scan, Voice Logging)
- Revenue growth from $102M (2020) to $345M (2024)
- Launch of 3-tier subscription model
- Acquisition of Intent (Feb 2025) for AI-powered meal planning
- Current valuation estimated at $750M (up from $345M purchase price)
- 2026 Winter Release: Photo Upload, enhanced Meal Planner with Recipes tab

### Data Breach (2018)

In February 2018, attackers compromised approximately **150 million MyFitnessPal accounts**, stealing usernames, email addresses, and hashed passwords. No financial data was exposed. In 2019, the stolen data appeared for sale on dark web marketplaces. This remains one of the largest data breaches for a fitness/health platform.

## 4. UX/UI Flow -- Screen-by-Screen Walkthrough

### 4.1 Onboarding Flow

**Step 1 -- Account Creation**
- Sign up options: Email, Facebook, Apple ID
- Minimal friction -- name, email, password
- Note: Known UX hiccup -- signing up with Facebook still asks for username/password

**Step 2 -- Goal Setting**
- "What is your goal?" screen with three options:
  - Lose weight
  - Gain weight
  - Maintain weight
- Selection determines the calorie calculation direction

**Step 3 -- Body Profile**
- Height input
- Current weight
- Goal weight
- Weekly weight change rate (0.5 lb/week to 2 lbs/week)

**Step 4 -- Activity Level**
- Not Very Active (sedentary/desk job)
- Lightly Active (light exercise 1-3 days/week)
- Active (moderate exercise 3-5 days/week)
- Very Active (hard exercise 6-7 days/week)

**Step 5 -- Calorie Target Calculation**
- App calculates BMR using Mifflin-St Jeor equation
- Applies activity multiplier
- Subtracts/adds deficit/surplus based on goal
- Displays daily calorie target (e.g., "Your daily goal: 1,800 calories")

**Step 6 -- App Tour**
- Progressive onboarding: step-by-step guide to key features
- Function-oriented: shows how to log food, track water, log exercise

### 4.2 Daily Dashboard (Today Tab)

The "Today" tab is the central hub:

- **Calorie summary ring:** Remaining calories = Goal - Food + Exercise
- **Macro progress bars:** Carbs, Protein, Fat (color-coded)
- **Food Diary:** Four meal slots (Breakfast, Lunch, Dinner, Snacks)
- **Water tracker:** Glass icons or numerical entry
- **Exercise summary:** Calories burned today
- **Steps counter:** Synced from connected devices
- **Quick-add button:** Blue (+) button at bottom center -- access to add food, water, weight, exercise, status update

### 4.3 Food Logging Flow

1. Tap (+) button or tap "Add Food" under a meal
2. **Unified search interface** presents three options:
   - **Search:** Type food name (autocomplete from 18M+ database)
   - **Scan:** Barcode scanner (Premium) or Meal Scan camera (Premium)
   - **Speak:** Voice logging (Premium) -- natural language input
3. Select food item from results (verified items show green checkmark)
4. **Portion selection screen:**
   - Serving size dropdown (cup, oz, gram, slice, etc.)
   - Number of servings (decimal supported)
   - Nutrition preview updates in real-time
5. **Confirm & Log** -- item added to diary under selected meal
6. Return to diary -- updated calorie/macro summary

### 4.4 Exercise Logging Flow

1. Tap (+) button > Exercise
2. Choose category: Cardiovascular or Strength
3. **Cardiovascular:** Search exercise > enter duration > calories auto-calculated based on body profile
4. **Strength Training:**
   - Search or select from Workout Routines
   - Enter sets, reps, weight per exercise
   - Option to save as routine for future use
5. Calories added back to daily food budget (configurable)

### 4.5 Progress Tracking Screens

- **Weight chart:** Line graph with trend over time (weekly, monthly, all-time views)
- **Progress photos:** Grid view with side-by-side comparison mode
- **Nutrition reports:** Weekly averages for calories, macros, micronutrients (Premium)
- **Weekly Digest:** Automated 7-day summary with insights
- **5-week projection:** When marking diary "complete," see projected weight in 5 weeks

### 4.6 Community & Social

- **Newsfeed:** Friend activity, completed diaries, milestones
- **Forums:** Categorized discussion boards (Getting Started, Food & Nutrition, Fitness, Motivation, etc.)
- **Friend finder:** Search by username or import from contacts
- **Diary sharing:** Choose to make food diary visible to friends or public

### 4.7 Settings & Integrations

- **Profile settings:** Update weight, height, activity level, goals
- **Diary settings:** Meal names (customizable), calorie/macro goals
- **Notifications:** Meal reminders, goal reminders, social notifications
- **Connected apps/devices:** 35+ integration options
- **Privacy controls:** Diary visibility, profile visibility
- **Account management:** Subscription, data export (Premium), delete account

## 8. Go-to-Market Strategy

### Early Mover Advantage (2005-2015)

MyFitnessPal's GTM was built on several compounding advantages:

1. **First-mover in digital calorie tracking:** Launched in 2005, years before the smartphone app boom
2. **SEO-driven organic growth:** The web platform captured search traffic for "calorie counter," "food calories," and thousands of food-specific queries
3. **Word-of-mouth virality:** The tool was so useful that doctors, dietitians, and trainers recommended it to patients/clients
4. **Zero marketing spend for years:** Bootstrapped growth meant every user was organic

### Food Database Moat

The single most important competitive advantage:

- **18+ million foods** in the database (largest in the world for consumer apps)
- **Crowdsourced expansion:** Users contribute new food entries, creating a network effect
- Started with USDA nutritional data, then expanded through user contributions
- Includes packaged goods (with barcodes), restaurant menus, generic/whole foods, international foods
- **Barcode database:** Millions of UPC codes mapped to nutritional data
- **Self-reinforcing:** More users --> more food entries --> better database --> more users

This database is nearly impossible to replicate from scratch. It represents 20 years of crowdsourced data accumulation.

### Partnership Strategy

- **Device integrations:** 35+ partnerships with Apple, Fitbit, Garmin, Samsung, etc.
- **Content partners:** Livestrong, shape.com, health publications
- **Influencer/creator partnerships:** TikTok creators, fitness influencers (measured via Branch Quick Links)
- **Healthcare:** Doctors and dietitians recommend the app as a tracking tool
- **Wearable/fitness ecosystem:** Positioned as the "nutrition layer" that connects to any fitness device

### Distribution Channels

1. **Organic search (web):** Massive SEO footprint for nutrition queries
2. **App store optimization:** Top-ranked for "calorie counter," "food tracker," "diet app"
3. **Professional referrals:** Healthcare providers, dietitians, personal trainers
4. **Social/influencer:** TikTok and Instagram partnerships
5. **Word of mouth:** Still the largest driver

## 11. Key Takeaways & Lessons

### Lesson 1: Data Moats Are Real -- But Not Forever

MyFitnessPal's 18M+ food database took 20 years to build through crowdsourcing. For a decade, this was an unassailable competitive advantage. No competitor could replicate it. However, AI and machine learning are beginning to erode this moat -- nutrition data can now be generated, verified, and scaled without relying solely on user contributions. **Takeaway: Data moats buy time, not permanence. Use the window to build additional moats (brand, switching costs, ecosystem).**

### Lesson 2: Bootstrapping Creates Extraordinary Discipline

Mike and Albert Lee ran MyFitnessPal with 4 employees serving 30 million users for years. They never raised outside capital for 8 years. This forced extreme capital efficiency and product focus. The $475M exit on zero outside funding represents one of the best bootstrapped outcomes in consumer tech history. **Takeaway: External funding is not required for massive scale. Constraint breeds innovation.**

### Lesson 3: Freemium at Scale Requires Careful Paywall Management

Under Francisco Partners, MyFitnessPal moved core features (barcode scanner, custom macros) behind the paywall. Revenue tripled ($102M to $345M), but user satisfaction cratered. 97% dissatisfaction with pricing. 67% unhappy with interface changes. The financial metrics look great, but the brand equity is being eroded. **Takeaway: Freemium paywalling works for revenue but has a trust cost. Move new features behind paywall, not existing free features.**

### Lesson 4: Acquisition Context Matters More Than Price

Under Armour paid $475M and couldn't monetize MyFitnessPal effectively (revenue was ~$86M by 2019). Francisco Partners paid $345M and tripled revenue in 4 years. The difference: PE firms specialize in monetization optimization; sportswear companies don't. **Takeaway: Who buys a product matters as much as the price. Alignment of capabilities to opportunity is everything.**

### Lesson 5: Network Effects in Content Databases

Every user who adds a food item makes the app better for all users. Every barcode scanned and verified extends the database. This creates a self-reinforcing growth loop that competitors cannot buy -- they have to earn it through usage volume over time. **Takeaway: Design your product so that usage itself improves the product for everyone. This is the strongest form of moat.**

### Lesson 6: Fitness vs. Nutrition Is a False Dichotomy

MyFitnessPal succeeded by straddling both fitness AND nutrition tracking. The integration with 35+ fitness devices positions it as the "nutrition layer" for any fitness stack. Users don't think in categories -- they think in goals (lose weight, get healthy). **Takeaway: Build for user goals, not industry categories. The integration point between fitness and nutrition is enormously valuable.**

### Lesson 7: The Logging Problem Is Unsolved

Despite 20 years of development, food logging is still manual and tedious. Barcode scanning helps for packaged foods, but home-cooked meals, restaurant dishes, and portion estimation remain friction-heavy. The company that truly solves passive or effortless food logging will disrupt this entire category. **Takeaway: The biggest opportunity in nutrition tracking is eliminating the logging burden entirely.**
